Interviewer (Dana): Thanks for the time, Marcus. Can you describe what Verdant Grid set out to build this year?

Subject (Marcus Lindqvist, CTO): Sure. We build controllers for residential solar microgrids — neighbourhoods where a lot of homes have rooftop solar and a battery, all tied together. This year's project was a predictive load-balancing controller. The goal was to keep voltage stable across the cluster while shifting load and battery charge/discharge around, so no single feeder gets overloaded when everyone's exporting at noon or pulling hard at dinner.

Dana: What made that hard? Couldn't you just use standard grid controls?

Marcus: The standard stuff assumes a central operator and predictable one-way power flow. Our situation is the opposite — power flows both directions, generation is intermittent, and there's no central meter we can trust in real time. The big unknown was whether we could forecast cluster-level net load accurately enough to act ahead of a voltage event, given how fast cloud cover changes generation. We genuinely didn't know if a useful forecast was even possible at the timescale we needed.

Dana: So what did you try?

Marcus: First we tried a rule-based controller — thresholds on voltage that triggered battery dispatch. It oscillated badly; batteries fought each other across homes and we got voltage flicker. So that approach told us coordination had to be predictive, not reactive. Then we built a forecasting model off historical generation and weather, and drove dispatch from the forecast. That was better in steady conditions but fell apart during fast partial-cloud transitions — the forecast lagged reality. The third iteration was a distributed approach where each home's controller negotiates with its neighbours instead of relying on a central forecast. That held voltage much better, but it introduced a new problem: the negotiation could fail to converge when comms latency spiked.

Dana: Where did it land?

Marcus: We've got the distributed negotiation working in simulation and on a small bench setup. Voltage stays within band in most scenarios. The open question we're still chasing is convergence guarantees under degraded communications — we don't yet know the conditions where it's provably stable. And honestly there's a lot of homeowner-app polish we did too, the dashboard and notifications, which took a chunk of time. But the core controller is the real work.

Dana: Anything on results?

Marcus: In sim, the distributed controller cut voltage excursions by about two-thirds versus the rule-based baseline. That's the headline. The convergence-under-latency thing is next year's problem.

Background reference notes (general domain context, not company-specific):

- Partial shading on a PV string causes "bypass diode activation," where shaded cells are bypassed and the string's output drops in characteristic steps. This is a well-documented cause of generation mismatch in residential arrays.
- For lithium battery systems, the industry-standard baseline for state-of-charge estimation error using coulomb-counting methods is typically around 3% under controlled conditions; real-world error is higher.
